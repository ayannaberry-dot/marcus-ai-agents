LinkedIn post draft · Week of Apr 28

Source moment: Apr 30 voice memo after the Patel inspection. Carrying real specificity, no client identifiers.

Draft

A buyer asked me yesterday whether they should waive their inspection contingency to win a bid. I said no. Then I said it again. Here's the thing: the case for waiving inspection is real. In a thin market, sellers see contingencies as risk. Waiving signals seriousness, can win you the property, can save you a price negotiation. I get it. But here's what I tell every buyer in their first home: a house is the most expensive thing you've ever bought, and the only one you'll never test-drive before signing. The inspection isn't there to win you renegotiation leverage. It's there so you walk in on day one knowing what you bought. There's a version of this advice that's "always inspect, no exceptions, full stop." That's not what I tell people. There's a real, specific scenario — a deal you'd write at 10% over your number, where the property is fully renovated by a builder you know, and your appetite for surprise is genuinely zero — where waiving inspection might be the right move. But that's a one-in-twenty deal. And in 2026's market, it's still being treated as a one-in-three move. If a competing offer is willing to waive inspection on a 1898 single-family they've toured for forty minutes, you don't have to match them. You just have to be the next buyer in line when their finance falls through. Patient capital wins more of these than people think. — M

  • Hook: The "I said no. Then I said it again." line.
  • Lens: Why the case for waiving sounds reasonable, so you're not strawmanning.
  • Point: The "one-in-twenty being treated as one-in-three" framing.
